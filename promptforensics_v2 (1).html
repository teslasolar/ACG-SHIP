Methodology

How Prompt Forensics Works

A four-stage analysis pipeline. From pasted material to a defensible hiring recommendation, with every step grounded in observable signal — not credentials, network, or self-reporting.

01
Paste Public Material
Submit the candidate's own words: posts, articles, build logs, repositories. No scraping, no fetching — you control the input and the candidate's privacy.
02
Forensic Analysis
Material is scored across nine AI-native fluency dimensions and matched against eight role archetypes. The composite score places the candidate on a five-tier milestone ladder from Entry to Co-Creator.
03
Pre-Screen Probe
A fifteen-minute challenge is auto-generated to probe the candidate's primary archetype and verify identified gaps. AI tools are explicitly permitted — this filters for AI-natives, not against them.
04
Trial Build Recommendation
For candidates who pass pre-screen, the report recommends an appropriate paid trial build tier — from 24-hour probe to week-long embed — representative of the actual role work.

Four Structural Failures

Why Conventional Hiring Cannot Solve This

These are not edge cases. They are systemic failures baked into the infrastructure most companies use to hire for roles that did not exist two years ago.

Failure 01
Keyword Filters Reject AI-Native Signal
Applicant Tracking Systems reject candidates whose CVs miss specific keyword matches — even when they are qualified. Nearly every major employer uses this approach. The best AI-native builders often do not use the tools the keyword lists are built around. They ship with sovereignty, choose stacks by fit, and describe work in their own vocabulary.
Data: 52% of keywords in a job description are missing from the average qualified candidate's CV. 99.7% of recruiters use ATS filtering. 88% of employers report losing qualified candidates to this process.
Failure 02
Ghost Jobs Poison the Signal
Roughly one in five job postings is a ghost job — posted with no intent to hire. 45% of HR professionals admit to posting them regularly. The consequence: candidates cannot tell real opportunities from signalling noise, and companies that are genuinely hiring compete with pipelines polluted by ghost listings from peers. Trust in the process collapses on both sides.
Data: 18–22% of U.S. job postings are ghost jobs. 93% of HR professionals engage in the practice to some degree. 30% of 2025 postings never resulted in a hire.
Failure 03
Recruiters Cannot Grade Work They Cannot Do
Most recruiters placing AI roles are not themselves AI-native. They match on credentials, titles, and years of experience — proxies that do not translate to AI work. An AI-native practitioner with two years of shipping production systems can be filtered out in favour of a candidate with eight years of experience in a superseded stack. The industry is attempting to evaluate a new category with tools built for the old one.
Data: 89% of candidates one year below the required experience threshold are auto-rejected. Most AI tools in production today did not exist when current seniority requirements were written.
Failure 04
The Interview Bans the Skill Being Hired For
A growing number of companies ban AI tools during technical interviews, claiming it is the only way to test "real" ability. This filters out precisely the candidates hiring managers wanted — the ones who ship fluently with AI every working day. Candidates who perform best without AI tools often underperform once hired, because the job itself requires the tools they were banned from demonstrating.
Data: 89% of executives believe conventional hiring processes need revision. Interview-to-hire conversion for AI roles is materially lower than other technical roles, despite higher starting salaries and stronger demand.

Example Analysis

Input and Output

A worked example. On the left, publicly available material from a candidate. On the right, the structured output our analysis produces — scored, ranked, and ready for hiring review.

Input — Candidate's Public Material

LinkedIn post · 340 words · Senior AI Engineer candidate

Shipped a new internal tool this week. Team was spending 40+ hours per month triaging customer feedback across five channels. Built a single-page classifier that pulls from all sources, categorises by theme and urgency, and routes to the right team — ran it on vanilla JS with Claude's API, stored classifications in IndexedDB.

Three interesting things came up during the build:

First, the prompt design. I started with a standard few-shot classification prompt and it was fine, maybe 82% accuracy on our eval set. Switched to giving the model the team's own taxonomy document as context instead of examples — let the model extrapolate from our actual categorisation rules. Accuracy jumped to 94%. Less prompt, more signal.

Second, the eval rigour. We graded the first 200 classifications manually and found the model was systematically mislabelling "billing confusion" as "billing complaint" — different severity, different team. Added that as a specific callout in the prompt and the error rate on that category went from 18% to under 3%. Worth actually reading the failure modes.

Third, the deployment. Colleagues wanted me to wrap it in Next.js and deploy to Vercel. I pushed back — it's a single HTML file, runs locally, costs nothing to host, and works offline. The team using it doesn't care about the stack, they care that it opens instantly. Sometimes the best infrastructure is no infrastructure.

Going forward, thinking about how to make this pattern reusable for other teams. The classifier logic is generic; only the taxonomy changes. Considering a lightweight template that any team could configure without touching the code.
